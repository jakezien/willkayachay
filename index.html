Willka Yachay, a Peruvian nonprofit, was founded to improve the Q'eros quality of life through education, enabling the next generation of Q'eros to become leaders who can guide their community toward sustainable modernity.


By elevating their standard of living while preserving their cultural identity, we give the Q'eros children the resources to retain roots in their traditional world, engage the modern world, and flourish.

Q'eros

(KEH-ros)

The Q'eros Nation has been designated by Peru's Ministry of Culture a "national living cultural patrimony" for the continuity of its ancient Andean traditions.


It is considered to be the "último ayllu inka," or the last Incan community of Peru. The Q'eros strive to preserve and promote their indigenous ethnic and cultural identity.

Q'eros live a hardworking life immersed in nature: they perform ritual offerings to Pacha Mama, Mother Earth, and to the Apus, mountain spirits, in exchange for the well being of their animals, crops and themselves.

Worldview concepts of ayni, the importance of reciprocal sharing, and animu, awareness of an animated essence in all things, shape their interactions with each other and their environment. Love, joy, perseverance, and an intrinsic integrity — the ability to "simply just be" — suffuse their daily lives.

As a testament to their character and warmth, and despite the harsh conditions to which they are exposed, and a history as targets of discrimination and marginalization, the Q'eros people endure.

Q'eros

Where
in the world

The remote villages of Q'eros are located at 14,500 feet above sea level in the snow-capped Cordillera Vilcanota range, the highest mountain chain in southeastern Peru. There are over 2,000 people who live in the nation's fourteen villages spread across many river valleys.

Q'eros travel between and utilize three different ecosystems that span from 15,500 feet down to 6,000 feet above sea level: from snowy peaks to ceja de selva, "the eyebrow of the jungle."


They raise llamas and alpacas at the highest altitudes, descend to grow potatoes and raise sheep, and descend further to grow corn, squash and other vegetables and to harvest bamboo and wood for buildings, looms and flutes.

Willka Yachay

(SACRED KNOWLEDGE)

Willka Yachay's vision is to enable indigenous peoples,
like Q' eros, to revitalize their cultures, develop their
communities, engage the modern world, and flourish.

The Q'eros people possess unique knowledge, from religion to farming to weaving. Yet their way of life is threatened. Many are migrating to urban areas for the sole purpose of educating their children. Families rarely return to the community after leaving. The Q'eros are striving to sustain their indigenous Andean identity in the face of outward migration, large-scale mining, globalization and the arrival of a road.

Willka Yachay and community members identified culturally relevant primary and secondary education in Q'eros as the first step toward a sustainable future for their children and society.


All of our projects are rooted in the United Nation's Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples concerning their culture, identity, language, employment, health and education. The declaration "emphasizes the rights of indigenous peoples to maintain and strengthen their own institutions, cultures and traditions, and to pursue their development in keeping with their own needs and aspirations."

Our Schools

Willka Yachay founded and sustains two community-based primary schools and a secondary school in the indigenous Q'eros Nation. The goal is to enable the next generation of Q'eros to become leaders who can guide their community toward sustainable modernity, elevating their standard of living while preserving their cultural identity.


Our alternative, ethnic curriculum strengthens their ancestral knowledge and wisdom. We encourage mothers and fathers to have an integral role in designing and supporting our innovative programming and projects.

Primary Schools

Willka Yachay coordinates all fundraising and programming for two community-based primary schools: Inkari, in the village of Ch'allma Chimpana, and Kusi Qoyllur, in Qocha Moqo. Qualified professors are teaching a combined total of forty children to read and write in both Quechua, their native tongue, and Spanish, and preparing them to interact with the modern world while preserving and nurturing their traditions. The warm-hearted and creative children are enjoying an innovative education that respects and cultivates their culture.

Ethnic High School

Willka Yachay's ethnic high school, or Colegio Etnico, in the village of Qocha Moqo, was founded in 2012 and is the first of its kind in Peru and perhaps in the world in that its curriculum is based on and nurtures a rich indigenous cultural heritage while teaching language and math skills through scientific, environmental and ethnographic projects. It is providing an education that will enable young Q'eros to go on to university, which none has ever done, and ultimately direct their community's future.


The high school's director, Jesús Rios Mencia, is a scientist with masters' degrees in mathematics and physics and the author of a book on Andean ancestral scientific and spiritual knowledge. He is deeply committed to indigenous rights and internationally recognized as a leader in culturally relevant education for indigenous populations.

  • Food Security

    The ethnic high school's first learning project was building a masonry oven that bakes bread and heats water for Qocha Moqo. Willka Yachay's vision is to support other villages in doing the same.


    Willka Yachay and community members are planning to build a trout pond and more greenhouses in 2013 to supplement the local diet of potatoes and alpaca with fresh fish and vegetables.

  • Weaving Cooperative

    Willka Yachay is helping the families of Ch'allma Chimpana form a cooperative to sell their beautiful traditional weavings at a just price, locally and abroad.

  • Music Preservation

    Student musicians and ethnomusicologist, Dr. Holly Wissler, are working together to recover, preserve and practice traditional songs based on their own ethnographic research. They perform at community events throughout the region.

  • Traditional Medicine

    Working with traditional Q'eros healers, or curanderos, primary and secondary students are learning about native medicinal plants and their uses. Willka Yachay's goal is to develop a community health center that will combine modern medicine and ancient wisdom.

  • Incan Mathematics

    The ethnic high school director, Jesús Rios Mencia, has researched and recovered the Incan mathematical device, Yupana, Quechua for “counting tool.” High school students have learned the system and teach it to younger students within the Q'eros Nation and at schools and universities in Cusco.

  • Alternative Energy

    The alternative energy projects we support are designed to teach mathematics, physics and engineering, and improve the quality of life in Q'eros.


    Willka Yachay's first successful energy project was a hydro-electric generator hand built and deployed by our high school students and their teacher. The turbine now provides electricity for several classrooms.

    We have supplied and installed one solar panel system and will implement more in the future with an eye toward bringing electricity to all of Q'eros.

Our Story

Hannah Rae Porst's work in Q'eros began in 2010 when she lived among its people while researching the intersection of culture and development. During a community dinner on the eve of her departure she asked the village parents how she could thank them for their hospitality. There was some initial talk about soccer shoes, but what they really needed, they told her, was a school. While a senior in college, she raised funds, founded and helped build the first primary school in the Q'eros village of Ch'allma Chimpana. Hannah moved to Cusco in 2011 to continue work with the Q'eros. She and a colleague, Heidi Helena, established Willka Yachay, a Peruvian non-profit organization. Willka Yachay is Quechua for sacred knowledge. Hannah loves the Q'eros people and continues to learn much from them.
